about the project
This project is for Cognitive Design Studio; students learn to integrate research into design & development and apply human-centered design through real-world opportunities.

Qualcomm Thinkabit Lab

Our design focus revolved around Qualcomm's Thinkabit Lab, on their Thursday "Tinkering Nights". It's a chance for Qualcomm employees to bring their family members in to play around with some of the technology Qualcomm has to offer, such as their Arduino boards.

The problem with the Thinkabit Lab is that it can be quite intimidating for newcomers and even returners who are unsure how to proceed. The lab space is poorly organized. Due to the small amount of hosts, people are not always greeted when they enter which leads to further confusion and intimidation. When this occurs people are much less likely to enjoy their experience and return in the following weeks.

Competitive Analysis

In the beginning, we had a very vague understanding of our design opportunity. We knew we would be helping Qualcomm employees engage in tinkering and building activities, but did not know the manner in which to do so. Therefore, we each conducted two competitive analyses and interviews to better understand the existing services and organizations that utilize a type of "makerspace". From this we learned about some existing features of similar makerspace environments that generate a beneficial and immersive experience.

The Needs

For our first trip, we wanted to obtain a better understanding of the problem areas within the lab. Therefore, we conducted several interviews with the employees to find out what they felt were the main issues. We also observed individuals while they were tinkering and navigating throughout the lab. After our initial findings, it became clear that there was a lack of general information on the Lab, as well as what materials were available to use. Ideation to Implementation

Having 10 weeks to complete this project, my team and I developed a pseudo-Agile approach to better streamline our goals. Each time we met at the Thinkabit Lab, the work was distributed in a way that provided equal attention to both research (i.e. interviews, observations) and prototype development. Aside from in-class discussions, we also designated 1-2 times to meet outside to update weekly progress via a GoogleDoc.

  2. Liked Products: The Liked Products section allows users to look back at items they were interested in, but perhaps did not intend to purchase at the moment they discovered it.
  3. Search: Specific browsing for items based on brand or design type (shirts, pants, etc.). Includes navigation into Product View, which provides users with information on the selected item such as personalized fitting style and user reviews.
  4. Shopping Cart: As with any app that provides an e-commerce experience, a shopping cart is available for users to keep track of to-be purchased items. IA follows that of a standard online-shopping format.
  5. Profile: Body preference settings are found here. Users can edit these preferences based on their needs and the app will respond accordingly, i.e. automatically filter results to be within user sizing parameters.

Design Details

Sizing Recommendations

Fitted provides personalized recommendations across all products. For each product view, the user gets an indication of how well the item is going to fit on their body (based on the preferences they provide).

Each item the user views will have a percentage that informs them of how accurate the fit will be based on their size. The option can be changed, giving users freedom and flexibility to choose how well the item should fit them. Reviews also further inform users because we understand the importance of having constant feedback from real-world experiences.

This is the bread and butter of Fitted. Users have the freedom to adjust their body profile based on their own preferences. Whether users want an overall tighter fit in the clothes they buy, or only in the torso length, they have the ability to indicate that and Fitted will filter and tailor their results accordingly.

Next Steps

There’s a lot I would like to do with future features. My ideal next steps would be: personalized styling recommendations (shoutout to Amazon Echo) and C2C marketplace capability for renting and selling unused clothing. Providing these features will allow for a better in-app experience, and I hope the data we collect will help validate these decisions.

The realm of online shopping is always expanding and having certain, personalized information at our fingertips can better help us in finding the right clothes.
